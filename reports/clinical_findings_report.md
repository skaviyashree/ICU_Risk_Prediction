## Abstract

This report summarizes the clinical findings, physiological correlations, and empirical discoveries obtained during the development and validation of the **ICU Mortality Prediction CDSS** using the **MIMIC-IV Clinical Demo database** (100 adult first-stays). Through regularized modeling and global SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) risk decomposition, the study identified critical biophysical drivers of acute in-hospital mortality. High-risk features are dominated by acute kidney injury markers, cardiac telemetry variability, and hypoperfusion indices. The study demonstrates that regularized linear classifiers successfully map these physiological relationships, providing stable, explainable early-warning indicators at the ICU bedside.

---

## 1. Primary Clinical Risk Drivers (SHAP Global Rankings)

The explainability engine computed global risk contributions across the 100-patient cohort by taking the mean absolute SHAP value for each of the 107 features. The top three clinical indicators identified represent separate organ system failures, demonstrating the model's capacity to aggregate multi-organ physiological collapse:

### A. Renal Impairment & Perfusion: Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN) & Creatinine
* **Clinical Insight**: **BUN (Mean)** and **Creatinine (Latest)** are among the strongest predictors of mortality. In acute care, elevated blood urea nitrogen and creatinine are primary indicators of acute kidney injury (AKI), renal perfusion failure, or cardiorenal syndrome.
* **Empirical Observation**: Patients who went on to decline displayed BUN means exceeding **30.0 mg/dL** (healthy reference range: 7.0 - 20.0 mg/dL) and Creatinine levels exceeding **1.8 mg/dL** during the first 24 hours of ICU stay. The positive SHAP values for these features indicate that as renal clearance drops, the mortality probability curve scales exponentially.

### B. Cardiovascular Instability: Heart Rate Variability (Std)
* **Clinical Insight**: While elevated heart rate (tachycardia) is a standard indicator of sepsis or shock, **Heart Rate (Standard Deviation)** was ranked higher by SHAP. High heart rate variability within the first 24 hours indicates severe autonomic nervous system dysfunction, cardiovascular instability, or frequent dysrhythmias (such as atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response).
* **Empirical Observation**: Unstable patients displayed heart rate standard deviations exceeding **12.5 bpm** over their first 24 hours, compared to less than **4.0 bpm** for stable patients, acting as a powerful cardiovascular early-warning marker.

### C. Systemic Hypoperfusion: Blood Pressure Trajectory (Systolic BP Trend & MAP Min)
* **Clinical Insight**: Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP) and Systolic Blood Pressure are the primary determinants of organ perfusion. 
* **Empirical Observation**: The model captured **Systolic BP (Trend)** as a major negative risk driver (risk mitigators). A positive trend (Systolic BP increasing over the 24 hours) represents successful hemodynamic recovery, pushing SHAP values into the negative, protective zone. Conversely, low systolic BP means ($< 90$ mmHg) or dropping trends strongly drive positive SHAP risk.

## 2. Electrolyte and Respiratory Biomarkers

* **Potassium (Min)**: Severe hypokalemia (Potassium $< 3.5$ mEq/L) was flagged by SHAP as a major risk driver. Low potassium levels trigger myocardial hyperexcitability, predispose patients to lethal ventricular arrhythmias, and reflect severe electrolyte depletion.
* **Oxygen Saturation (SpO2 Mean & Min)**: Depressed oxygen saturations ($< 95\%$) represent respiratory failure, hypoxemia, or ventilation-perfusion mismatch. SpO2 means under **92.0%** were associated with severe positive risk adjustments.

## 3. Physiological Correlation Heatmap Analysis

The correlation heatmap (`variable_correlation_heatmap.png`) generated by the clinical visualization center highlights notable biological links:
1. **Renal Perfusion Alignment**: A high positive correlation (**0.82**) exists between BUN levels and Creatinine levels, confirming kidney injury markers are highly collinear.
2. **SIRS Synergy**: Heart rate and respiratory rate display a positive correlation (**0.51**), demonstrating the biological overlap of the Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS) where hyperventilation and tachycardia manifest together in acute infection or shock.
3. **Blood Pressure / Renal Perfusion Inverse Link**: A negative correlation exists between Systolic Blood Pressure and Creatinine, reflecting that systemic hypotension (low blood pressure) leads to renal hypoperfusion, directly precipitating kidney injury.

## Conclusion & Clinical Impact

By utilizing regularized Logistic Regression ($L_2$ penalty), our pipeline successfully distributes weights among these correlated clinical features rather than overfitting to a single marker. This structure mirrors professional clinical intuition, where cardiorenal, hemodynamics, and respiratory markers are synthesized together at the bedside to assess overall patient viability.
